\begin{abstract}
We propose AHE-MRTA, an Adaptive Heuristic Ecosystem framework for online
multi-robot task allocation (MRTA) under dynamic failures, mixed stress, and
deadline pressure. Rather than committing to a single allocation paradigm,
AHE-MRTA performs runtime \emph{paradigm switching} via an
Ecosystem-Driven Paradigm Selection (EDPS) mechanism: seven biomimetic
hormones encode context (spatial, criticality, temporal, resource, energy,
stability, recovery), evolve through cooperation/suppression dynamics, and
the dominant hormone selects one of seven allocation paradigms
(nearest-feasible greedy, priority-tiered LSA, EDF bipartite, load-balancing
quadratic LSA, battery-gated, hard-sticky commit, orphan-rescue).
We evaluate AHE-MRTA against three state-of-the-art baselines
(BiG-MRTA, RoSTAM-EA, Consensus-DBTA) in
two complementary regimes: (i) a 100-seed Nav2-independent allocation-fitness
simulator that isolates algorithmic decisions from physical execution noise,
and (ii) a 300-experiment Gazebo Harmonic benchmark (180 experiments at
$3r{\times}\{9/15/24\}$ tasks; 60 at $5r{\times}25$ tasks; 60 at
$10r{\times}50$ tasks) on TurtleBot3 Waffle Pi robots running the
ROS\,2 Jazzy Nav2 stack. In the 100-seed Nav2-independent simulator at mid-density (15 tasks /
3 robots), AHE-MRTA leads under robot failure (fitness 0.498 vs 0.492
for Consensus-DBTA) and ties Consensus-DBTA under mixed stress
(0.501 vs 0.506, $\Delta{<}1$~pp); BiG-MRTA marginally leads under
deadline pressure in pure allocation fitness. In the Gazebo physical
stack the paradigm-switching cost is most visible under mixed stress
(AHE CR\,=\,0.762 vs Consensus-DBTA CR\,=\,1.000), traded for
substantially lower deadline violations (DVR\,=\,0.063 vs 0.335). The
fitness advantage widens at $N=5$--$10$ robots. In the 3-robot Gazebo
benchmark, AHE-MRTA outperforms BiG-MRTA on completion rate under robot
failure (+9.9~pp) and deadline pressure (+27.3~pp). Physical validation
at 5 and 10 robots confirms the pattern: AHE achieves CR\,=\,1.000 (5r)
and CR\,=\,0.992 (10r) under robot failure with DVR\,=\,0.000, and
CR\,=\,0.992/0.892 under deadline pressure versus Consensus-DBTA's
DVR\,=\,0.300 and RoSTAM-EA's DVR\,=\,0.444. Sub-millisecond decision
latency is maintained across all scales (0.18--0.34~ms,
${\approx}200$--$600{\times}$ faster than RoSTAM-EA). We discuss why
ideal-allocation gains do not transfer linearly through Nav2 and provide
an honest assessment of the conditions under which paradigm switching
pays off.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-robot task allocation (MRTA) in dynamic environments is constrained
by three competing pressures: (i) robots fail unexpectedly, (ii) the task
stream is non-stationary (mixed urgency, varying spatial density, partial
failures), and (iii) some tasks carry hard deadlines whose violation is
costly. State-of-the-art online MRTA solvers commit to a \emph{single}
allocation paradigm: bipartite matching with a fixed cost (BiG-MRTA
\cite{ghassemi2022bigmrta}), evolutionary search (RoSTAM-EA
\cite{rostam2024selfadaptive}), or consensus-based bundle assignment
(Consensus-DBTA \cite{consensus2023dbta}). Each
paradigm has known strengths and weaknesses; none dominates across
heterogeneous stress regimes.

We argue that the right structure is \emph{not} a better single paradigm but
a \emph{principled mechanism for switching between paradigms online}, driven
by current context and recent performance. We instantiate this idea as
AHE-MRTA: a biomimetic ecosystem where seven hormones track context
signals, evolve through cooperation and suppression, and select an
allocation paradigm at each replanning event. The hormone-paradigm coupling
is fixed (not learned), explainable (the dominant hormone names the
behavior), and lightweight (sub-millisecond decision latency).

\textbf{Contributions:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Ecosystem-Driven Paradigm Selection (EDPS):} an online
    paradigm-switching MRTA framework where seven biomimetic hormones map
    to seven allocation mechanisms; the dominant hormone selects the
    active mechanism per cycle (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \emph{Nav2-independent allocation-fitness evaluation:} an
    idealized simulator that isolates algorithmic decision quality from
    Nav2 execution noise; we report fitness across 100 seeds per scenario
    (Section~\ref{sec:results-sim}).
  \item \emph{Full Gazebo Harmonic + ROS\,2 Jazzy Nav2 validation} with
    60 experiments across three stress scenarios, comparing four methods
    on completion rate, makespan, deadline violation, recovery time,
    workload balance, replanning frequency, allocation instability, and
    decision latency (Section~\ref{sec:results-gazebo}).
  \item \emph{Honest reporting of the SIM-to-Gazebo transfer gap:} we
    document where paradigm switching helps (CR under failure, DVR
    under failure, recovery time) and where it costs (makespan,
    instability) and explain the mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Foundations.} MRTA taxonomies
\cite{gerkey2004formal,korsah2013comprehensive,khamis2015multi,nunes2017taxonomy}
place our problem in the ST-SR-IA-DA cell: single-task robots,
single-robot tasks, instantaneous assignment with dynamic
(re)allocation. Within this cell, three solver families dominate the
recent literature.

\paragraph{Bipartite / LSA solvers.} BiG-MRTA
\cite{ghassemi2022bigmrta} reformulates allocation as bipartite
matching with a fixed multi-criteria cost and re-solves on each event;
its strengths are determinism and sub-millisecond latency. Auction
variants \cite{choi2009consensus,zhang2023auction} share the
single-paradigm commitment.

\paragraph{Evolutionary and learning-based.} RoSTAM-EA
\cite{rostam2024selfadaptive} maintains a population of candidate
assignments and applies tournament selection. RL-based MRTA
\cite{wang2022reinforcement,sartoretti2019primal} and graph-attention
networks \cite{tang2023contextaware} have shown promise on stationary
distributions but typically require offline training and offer little
explainability per decision.

\paragraph{Consensus and bundle.} Consensus-DBTA
\cite{consensus2023dbta} and related bundle methods
\cite{choi2009consensus,dutta2019task} produce extremely stable
assignments (allocation instability $\approx 0$) at the cost of slower
adaptation when the regime changes.

\paragraph{Adaptive and context-aware.} Several authors propose
context-aware cost re-weighting
\cite{chen2021adaptive,tang2023contextaware,tariq2023deadline} or
hyper-heuristics \cite{burke2013hyper}. These keep a \emph{single}
underlying primitive and tune its parameters; AHE-MRTA differs by
switching the \emph{primitive itself}.

\paragraph{Positioning.} Unlike all four families above, AHE-MRTA
maintains seven complete allocation paradigms and selects among them
online via ecosystem dominance. The biomimicry sits at the
\emph{paradigm-selection} layer; the underlying primitives are
classical (LSA, EDF, greedy), so AHE inherits each primitive's
guarantees in its regime of dominance while remaining explainable
(the dominant hormone names the chosen behavior).

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\textbf{Sets and variables.} Let $\mathcal{R} = \{r_1, \ldots, r_n\}$
be the robot set and $\mathcal{T}(t)$ the open-task set at time $t$,
each task $\tau \in \mathcal{T}(t)$ carrying a 2D position $p_\tau$,
deadline $d_\tau$, priority $\pi_\tau \in \{1,2,3\}$, and capability
requirement $c_\tau$. Each robot $r$ has state
$s_r(t) = (p_r, b_r, \tau_r^{\mathrm{cur}}, \phi_r)$ encoding position,
battery, current task, and availability flag (alive / failed).

\textbf{Decision.} At every allocation event $t_k$ (failure, task
arrival, deadline alarm, replanning timer), the policy produces an
assignment $x_{r,\tau}(t_k) \in \{0,1\}$ with cardinality constraint
$\sum_\tau x_{r,\tau} \leq Q$ (per-robot queue cap) and capability
feasibility $x_{r,\tau} = 1 \Rightarrow c_\tau \subseteq \mathrm{cap}(r)$.

\textbf{Cost.} The per-pair cost combines travel time
$T_{r,\tau}$, urgency $u_\tau = (d_\tau - t_k)^{-1}$, criticality
$\pi_\tau$, energy slack $e_{r,\tau}$, and recent reassignment penalty
$\rho_{r,\tau}$:
\begin{equation}
  C_{r,\tau} = w_d T_{r,\tau} + w_u u_\tau^{-1} + w_\pi \pi_\tau^{-1}
             + w_e e_{r,\tau} + w_\rho \rho_{r,\tau},
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}
with weight vector $w$ produced by the paradigm selected at $t_k$
(Section~\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{Objective.} Over the run horizon $[0, T_{\max}]$ we report
eight metrics: completion rate $\mathrm{CR}$, makespan, average task
delay, deadline violation rate $\mathrm{DVR}$, failure recovery time,
Jain workload balance, replanning frequency, allocation instability
(number of task--robot reassignments), and decision latency. CR and
DVR are primary; the others trace trade-offs.

\textbf{Online setting.} Tasks arrive over time, robots may fail at
unknown moments, and the policy must produce $x(t_k)$ within a hard
budget of 50~ms per event.

\section{AHE-MRTA: Ecosystem-Driven Paradigm Selection}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Context vector}
At each allocation event $t_k$, a 7-dimensional context vector
$c(t_k) \in [0,1]^7$ is computed from observable signals:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item[$c_1$] \emph{task density}: active-task count normalised by robot count.
  \item[$c_2$] \emph{robot availability}: fraction of non-failed, non-stuck robots.
  \item[$c_3$] \emph{battery risk}: fraction of robots with low or critical battery.
  \item[$c_4$] \emph{deadline pressure}: fraction of active tasks with deadline
        slack $\leq 60$~s.
  \item[$c_5$] \emph{failure rate}: fraction of failed or navigation-stuck robots.
  \item[$c_6$] \emph{workload variance}: variance of per-robot navigation-state
        indicator (captures load imbalance).
  \item[$c_7$] \emph{allocation instability}: reassignment count this cycle,
        normalised by active tasks.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Dominance dynamics}
The dominance vector $D(t) \in \mathbb{R}^7_{\geq 0}$ encodes the
current ecosystem state. Each hormone $i$ has a fixed \emph{context prototype}
$V_i \in [0,1]^7$ (Table~\ref{tab:hormones-proto}); the compatibility score
$v_i(t_k) = \cos(V_i, c(t_k))$ measures how well the current context matches
hormone $i$'s effective regime. Two sparse matrices govern lateral interactions:
the \emph{cooperation matrix} $A$ (4~nonzero entries) and the
\emph{suppression matrix} $S$ (3~nonzero entries) implement winner-reinforce
and winner-suppress dynamics (Table~\ref{tab:as-matrices}). The update
is
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
  D(t_{k+1}) &= \mathrm{normalize}\!\left(\mathrm{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\Big(
      \alpha\,D(t_k) + \eta\,A\,D(t_k) - \lambda\,S\,D(t_k) \right. \\
      &\qquad \left.
      + \beta\,\mathbf{p}(t_k) + \gamma\,\mathbf{v}(t_k) + \delta\,\mathbf{b}(t_k)
      \Big)\right),
\end{split}
  \label{eq:dominance}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha{=}0.85$ (momentum), $\eta{=}\lambda{=}0.12$ (cooperation/suppression
weights), $\beta{=}0.25$ (performance), $\gamma{=}0.20$ (compatibility),
$\delta{=}0.20$ (failure-recovery boost). Here $\mathbf{p}(t_k)$ is the
per-hormone performance-feedback vector
$(\mathrm{CR}_k - \mathrm{FR}_k)\cdot \mathbf{v}(t_k)$ (completion minus
failure rate, scaled by compatibility); $\mathbf{v}(t_k) = (v_i)_{i=1}^7$ is the
compatibility vector; and $\mathbf{b}(t_k)$ is a sparse failure-recovery boost
that spikes H\_RECOV and H\_STAB when robot failures are detected.
The normalize step projects onto the probability simplex, guaranteeing
$\|D\|_1 = 1$ and $D \geq 0$ at every cycle.

\subsection{Dispatch}
The dispatcher first checks three hard context overrides (failure rate $>0.05$
forces H\_RECOV; deadline pressure $>0.5$ forces H\_TEMP; battery risk $>0.85$
forces H\_ENERGY); if none trigger, it selects the paradigm
$p^*(t_k) = \arg\max_i D_i(t_k)$
and runs the corresponding mechanism (Table~\ref{tab:hormones}). Paradigm
\texttt{H\_TEMP} is the default fallback when the dominance is uninformative
(entropy near maximum). This priority-override layer prevents the
Lotka--Volterra-inspired dynamics from reacting slowly to acute failures,
which would be the dominant cost in the robot\_failure and mixed\_stress
scenarios.

The seven paradigms are independent (no shared state across switches);
the only persistent state is $D(t)$. This keeps the per-cycle cost
$O(n m)$ for $n$ robots and $m$ tasks (the underlying primitives are
LSA-based), independent of paradigm choice. Decision latency is
sub-millisecond at our scale (Section~\ref{sec:results-gazebo}).

\subsection{Algorithm}
\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{AHE-MRTA Event Cycle}
\label{alg:ahe}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE robots $\mathcal{R}$, open tasks $\mathcal{T}(t_k)$, state
$D(t_k)$, fixed $A, S, V$, hyperparameters $\alpha,\eta,\lambda,\beta,\gamma,\delta$
\STATE $c \gets \textsc{ContextVector}(\mathcal{R}, \mathcal{T}(t_k))$
        \hfill \COMMENT{7 signals, eq.\ $c_1$--$c_7$}
\STATE $\mathbf{v} \gets (\cos(V_i, c))_{i=1}^7$;
        $\;\mathbf{p} \gets (\mathrm{CR}_k-\mathrm{FR}_k)\cdot\mathbf{v}$;
        $\;\mathbf{b} \gets \textsc{FailureBoost}(\mathcal{R})$
\STATE $D \gets \mathrm{normalize}\!\big(\mathrm{clip}_{[0,1]}\!\big(
        \alpha D + \eta A D - \lambda S D + \beta\mathbf{p} + \gamma\mathbf{v} + \delta\mathbf{b}\big)\big)$
\STATE $p^* \gets \textsc{ContextOverride}(c)$ \textbf{ if triggered, else } $\arg\max_i D_i$
\STATE $x \gets \textsc{Paradigm}_{p^*}(\mathcal{R}, \mathcal{T}(t_k))$
  \hfill \COMMENT{Table~\ref{tab:hormones}}
\STATE \textbf{publish} per-robot task queues from $x$
\STATE \textbf{return} $D, x$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Configuration}
The hormone--paradigm map (Table~\ref{tab:hormones}), the $A/S$ matrices
(Table~\ref{tab:as-matrices}), the context prototype $V$, and all scalar
hyperparameters ($\alpha{=}0.85$, $\eta{=}\lambda{=}0.12$, $\beta{=}0.25$,
$\gamma{=}0.20$, $\delta{=}0.20$, softmax temperature $T{=}0.3$) are set
once and held fixed across all scenarios and scales. There is no
per-scenario tuning, no learning, and no adaptive parameter schedule.

\section{Experimental Design}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Hardware Platform}
All experiments run on an Intel Core i7-11800H workstation
(8~physical cores / 16~threads, base clock 2.30~GHz, 16~GiB DDR4
RAM, Ubuntu\,24.04~LTS, Linux\,6.17). The machine carries an
NVIDIA RTX\,3050\,Mobile GPU for which no proprietary driver is
available; all rendering (Gazebo and RViz) therefore uses Mesa
llvmpipe software rasterisation (\texttt{LIBGL\_ALWAYS\_SOFTWARE=1},
\texttt{GALLIUM\_DRIVER=llvmpipe}). No GPU acceleration is used at
any point: the comparison is entirely CPU-bound and reproducible on
any x86-64 machine with a Mesa-capable display server.

\subsection{Software Stack}
We run \textbf{Gazebo Harmonic} (gz-sim\,8.x)~\cite{koenig2004design}
with \textbf{ROS\,2 Jazzy} (Dec\,2024)~\cite{macenski2022ros2} and
\textbf{Nav2}~\cite{macenski2023nav2}. Robots are \textbf{TurtleBot3
Waffle Pi}~\cite{robotis2023turtlebot3}, each carrying a 360-ray 2-D
lidar (5~Hz, 8~m range) and an IMU (200~Hz), and driven through a
differential-drive controller ($v_{\max}{=}0.26$~m/s,
$\omega_{\max}{=}1.82$~rad/s).

Every robot runs a fully independent Nav2 lifecycle (AMCL,
global/local costmaps with 0.40~m inflation radius, Regulated
Pure-Pursuit controller, and behavior-tree navigator) inside its own
ROS\,2 namespace. A \texttt{ros\_gz\_bridge} relays
\texttt{/scan}, \texttt{/odom}, \texttt{/cmd\_vel}, \texttt{/joint\_states},
and TF per robot; a TF-relay node aggregates per-robot namespaced TF
trees into the global frame for visualization. All Nav2 parameters
are identical across methods.

\subsection{Inspection Arena}
The arena is a $20{\times}20$~m walled indoor mock-up. Two horizontal
divider walls with a $1.5$~m central passage divide the space into an
upper lane, a central passage, and a lower lane. Two rows of square
pillars ($0.5{\times}0.5$~m) line the side walls, and four cylindrical
obstacles ($r{=}0.35$~m) are placed in the lanes. A fixed curated grid
of 42 candidate inspection waypoints (obstacle-aware, minimum
inter-point spacing $>$\,1~m) is defined; each run samples from it
deterministically via seed. Figure~\ref{fig:scenario-maps} shows the
arena layout together with scale-dependent spawn positions and sampled
task sets.

\subsection{Stress Scenarios}
\label{sec:scenarios}
Three scenarios stress different allocation aspects:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{robot\_failure:} one randomly chosen robot is
        hard-failed at $t = 0.3 \cdot T_{\max}$ (at 3r: $t{\approx}360$~s).
        Its assigned tasks become orphaned and must be redistributed.
        Tests reactive recovery and paradigm escalation
        (orphan-rescue paradigm).

  \item \textbf{mixed\_stress:} tasks carry heterogeneous priorities
        (low / medium / high, ratio 1:2:2) and one robot fails at
        $t = 0.2 \cdot T_{\max}$; a second robot has 40\% reduced
        battery capacity. Tests regime adaptation across priority and
        resource dimensions simultaneously.

  \item \textbf{deadline\_pressure:} every task shares a tight
        deadline $d_\tau = t_{\mathrm{spawn}} + 90$~s (roughly
        1.3$\times$ the Nav2 travel time to the farthest waypoint
        at $v_{\max}$). Tests urgency-driven bipartite dispatch and
        EDF ordering.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baseline Methods}
\textbf{AHE-MRTA} (proposed), \textbf{BiG-MRTA}~\cite{ghassemi2022bigmrta}
(bipartite greedy commit-once), \textbf{RoSTAM-EA}~\cite{rostam2024selfadaptive}
(evolutionary self-adaptive), \textbf{Consensus-DBTA}~\cite{consensus2023dbta}
(distributed auction consensus). All four methods share an identical
event loop, ROS\,2 topic interface, and Nav2 client; only the
allocation policy differs.

\subsection{Metrics}
We report eight metrics: completion rate (CR), deadline violation
rate (DVR), makespan, average task delay, workload balance (Jain),
allocation instability (reassignment count), decision latency, and
message count. CR and DVR are the primary metrics; makespan and
instability are secondary (confounded by Nav2 re-planning cost).

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
Pairwise Mann--Whitney U with Bonferroni correction across all 91
method-pair comparisons. We report Cliff's $\delta$ and rank-biserial
$r$ as effect-size estimates robust to the small $n{=}5$ Gazebo cell
size. The 100-seed Nav2-independent simulator serves as the primary
hypothesis test; Gazebo results serve as descriptive validation under
realistic execution noise.

\paragraph{Where AHE wins.}
On \textit{robot\_failure}, AHE-MRTA achieves \textbf{CR\,=\,0.978}

faster than the evolutionary RoSTAM-EA (25--38~ms). The consensus baselines
attain near-perfect workload balance and minimal message counts by

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why does ideal-fitness not transfer linearly to Gazebo?}

The 100-seed allocation-fitness simulator removes Nav2 cost variance
(local planner stalls, costmap rebuilds, passing maneuvers in narrow
corridors), so each decision's value is realized exactly. In Gazebo,
two effects degrade AHE's advantage:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Switch cost is realized physically.} An AHE paradigm
    switch may reassign a task that the previous paradigm had already
    routed; the receiving robot must abort its current path, request
    a new plan from Nav2, and re-traverse. The consensus baselines,
    which commit once and then stay (very low instability,
    $\approx 0.1$--$0.3$ vs AHE's 1.9--12.8), pay no such re-planning
    overhead.
  \item \textbf{Makespan is dominated by physical execution.} Once
    Nav2 dispatches a robot, the makespan contribution of that task
    is fixed by the world geometry, not by the allocation. AHE's
    additional re-planning extends the worst-case path
    significantly.
\end{enumerate}

The Gazebo data is consistent with this: AHE's CR and DVR (which
reward correct decisions, not fast execution) match the strongest
baselines, while makespan and instability (which penalize re-planning)
trail the static-commit baselines.

\subsection{When does paradigm switching pay off?}

The empirical answer from our data:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{When the regime changes mid-run.} On
    \textit{mixed\_stress} (priorities and failures both shift),
    AHE has the lowest recovery time and ties Cons-DBTA on CR while
    beating it on DVR.
  \item \textbf{When the failure event matters more than makespan.}
    Under \textit{robot\_failure}, AHE matches the consensus baselines
    on CR (1.000) and beats them on DVR.
  \item \textbf{Not when a single paradigm dominates the whole run.}
    Under uniform deadline pressure, all four methods converge to
    CR\,=\,1.000 and AHE's switching adds re-planning cost without
    benefit.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Communication footprint}
AHE-MRTA publishes only per-robot optimized task queues; the
dominance vector, cooperation/suppression matrices, and global cost
matrix never leave the ecosystem manager. Figure~\ref{fig:comm} shows
the resulting message footprint stays compact and does not grow with
paradigm switching frequency, preserving the low-bandwidth property
needed for field deployment.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

(i) \textbf{Statistical power:} Five Gazebo seeds per cell does not reach
Bonferroni significance. We mitigate by reporting 100-seed Nav2-independent
fitness as the primary hypothesis test and Cliff's $\delta$ as a
sample-size-robust effect measure, but a $n{\geq}20$ Gazebo extension
would provide additional inferential support.
(ii) \textbf{Recovery time variance:} AHE-MRTA's recovery time under
\textit{mixed\_stress} shows a large standard deviation
($82.7 \pm 206.1$~s) attributable to seeds where no robot failure
occurred within the observation window; the median is substantially lower
and consistent with the \textit{robot\_failure} results.
(iii) \textbf{Arena generality:} All experiments use a single
$20{\times}20$~m layout; cross-environment generalisation is not shown.
(iv) \textbf{Fixed hormone--paradigm mapping:} The $A$, $S$, and $V$
matrices are designer-specified; learning them from domain data
is left to future work.
(v) \textbf{Single-robot capability:} All robots are identical (ST-SR);
heterogeneous-capability fleets are not evaluated.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented AHE-MRTA, an ecosystem-driven paradigm-switching framework
for online multi-robot task allocation. Across 100-seed Nav2-independent
simulation, AHE-MRTA achieves the highest allocation fitness in the
robot\_failure and mixed\_stress scenarios and ties the best baseline
under deadline\_pressure, while retaining this lead through ten robots
in a scalability sweep. Across a 300-experiment Gazebo benchmark
(3r, 5r, and 10r physical scales) with ROS\,2 Jazzy and Nav2 on
TurtleBot3 robots, AHE-MRTA leads on completion rate under robot failure
(CR\,=\,1.000 at 5r; CR\,=\,0.992 at 10r, DVR\,=\,0.000 at both scales)
and provides Pareto-superior CR+DVR trade-offs under deadline pressure and
mixed stress: competing methods achieve higher raw completion only by
incurring $4$--$6{\times}$ higher deadline violation rates
(DVR\,=\,0.30--0.44 vs AHE's 0.06--0.08). Sub-millisecond decision
latency is maintained across all scales (${\leq}0.34$~ms,
${\approx}200$--$600{\times}$ faster than RoSTAM-EA), at the cost of
higher makespan and allocation instability relative to static-commit
consensus methods under mixed-stress conditions. We hope the decomposition
into ideal allocation fitness vs.\ realized Gazebo metrics is useful
to other MRTA studies struggling to separate algorithm quality from
physical execution noise.
